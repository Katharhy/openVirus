Abstract

In the late autumn of 2019, aÂ new potentially lethal human coronavirus designated severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirusÂ 2 (SARS-CoV-2) emerged in Wuhan, China. The pandemic spread of this zoonotic virus has created aÂ global health emergency and an unprecedented socioeconomic crisis. The severity of coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19), the illness caused by SARS-CoVâ€‘2, is highly variable. Most patients (~85%) develop no or mild symptoms, while others become seriously ill, some succumbing to disease-related complications. In this review, the SARS-CoVâ€‘2 life cycle, its transmission and the clinical and immunological features of COVID-19 are described. In addition, an overview is presented of the virological assays for detecting ongoing SARS-CoVâ€‘2 infections and the serological tests for SARS-CoV-2-specific antibody detection. Also discussed are the different approaches to developing aÂ COVID-19 vaccine and the perspectives of treating COVID-19 with antiviral drugs, immunomodulatory agents and anticoagulants/antithrombotics. Finally, the cardiovascular manifestations of COVID-19 are briefly touched upon. While there is still much to learn about SARS-CoVâ€‘2, the tremendous recent advances in biomedical technology and knowledge and the huge amount of research into COVID-19 raise the hope that aÂ remedy for this disease will soon be found. COVID-19 will nonetheless have aÂ lasting impact on human society.

Introduction

Coronaviruses (CoVs) were first described in the 1930s and first visualised (by electron microscopy) in the 1960s [1, 2]. CoVs own their name to the pedal-shaped protrusions emerging from the surface of the virus particles (also called virions), which give them an appearance reminiscent of aÂ crown. CoVs belong to the family of Coronaviridae, which is divided into two subfamilies. The main subfamilyâ€"Orthocoronavirinaeâ€"is subdivided into four genera, designated Alphacoronavirus, Betacoronavirus, Gammacoronavirus and Deltacoronavirus. CoVs generally have aÂ limited host range and, with aÂ few exceptions, cause either respiratory or enteric infections in mammals and birds. Until recently, six different human CoVs were distinguished, all of which primarily infect the respiratory tract (Tab.Â 1). Of these viruses, HCoV-229E, HCoV-HKU1, HCoV-OC43 and HCoV-NL63 are responsible for some 10â€"15% of the annual common colds [3]. Whereas infections with these viruses usually take aÂ mild course this is not the case for infections with severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS)-CoV and Middle East respiratory syndrome (MERS)-CoV, for which case-fatality rates of ~10 and ~35%, respectively, have been reported [4].

Recently, aÂ new human CoV has emerged in Wuhan, Peopleâ€™s Republic of China, which appears genetically very closely related to several bat CoV isolates and somewhat more distantly to SARS-CoV [5, 6]. Based on phylogenetic considerations, the new virus was named SARS-CoVâ€‘2, which stands for severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirusÂ 2 [7], and the accompanying disease was designated coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19). Like SARS-CoV and MERS-CoV, SARS-CoVâ€‘2 is assumed to be aÂ zoonotic virus with bats serving as reservoir hosts. Transmission of SARS-CoV and MERS-CoV from bats to humans likely occurred via intermediate hosts (presumably civet cats in the case of SARS-CoV and dromedary camels for MERS-CoV) to allow these viruses to adapt in aÂ stepwise fashion to their new host species. Spillover of SARS-CoVâ€‘2 from bats to humans probably also involved an intermediate host. Whether pangolins have acted as such, as initially proposed, is, however, doubtful [8]. The extraordinary ability of RNA viruses to adapt to changing selective pressures and to invade new hosts stems from the low fidelity with which they replicate their genome (i.e. genetic material) in combination with their high replication rate (i.e. short generation time). As aÂ consequence, the genomes of RNA viruses are highly heterogeneous, consisting of dynamic populations of genetically related variants, also known as mutant swarms or quasispecies [9]. This not only allows RNA viruses to infect new host species but also to escape immune surveillance and to become resistant to antiviral drugs.

Coronavirus life cycle

CoV particles are roughly spherical, have aÂ diameter of 120â€"160â€¯nm and consist of aÂ core (also called nucleocapsid) surrounded by aÂ protective coat or envelope (Fig.Â 1; [10]). The core contains the viral genome complexed with the nucleocapsidÂ (N)Â protein. CoV genomes consist of aÂ single RNA molecule of positive polarity with aÂ length of ~26.4 to ~31.7Â kilobases (kb). The envelope is composed of aÂ lipid bilayer (like the one found at the surface of cells) in which several transmembrane proteins are inserted. For SARS-CoVâ€‘2 the major envelope proteins are the spikeÂ (S) protein, the membraneÂ (M) protein and the envelopeÂ (E) protein. TheÂ SÂ protein is involved in the binding of the virus to target cells and has aÂ crucial role in the penetration of these cells by mediating membrane fusion. After binding of SARS-CoVâ€‘2 particles to its cellular receptor angiotensin IÂ converting enzymeÂ 2 (ACE2) and cleavage of the SÂ protein by transmembrane serine proteaseÂ 2 (TMPRSS2), the viral envelope fuses with the plasma membrane of the target cell, resulting in the delivery of the viral genome inside the cell (Fig.Â 2; [11, 12]). Alternatively, upon binding of their SÂ protein to ACE2, SARS-CoVâ€‘2 particles are taken up by the target cell in small vesicles called endosomes. Next, the SÂ protein is cleaved by the endosomal protease cathepsinÂ L, which initiates the fusion of the viral envelope with the lipid bilayer of the endosome, causing the release of the viral genome into the cytoplasm of the cell. AÂ recent paper suggested that SARS may utilise CD147 (also known as basigin or EMMPRIN) as an alternative attachment receptor to enter target cells [13]. After the CoV genome, which is actually aÂ very long mRNA [(+)gRNA], is set free inside the cell, it is immediately translated to produce two large polyproteins designated pp1a and pp1ab (Fig.Â 1). Synthesis of pp1ab requires the translation machinery to switch reading frame before encountering the stop codon of open reading frame (ORF) 1a by aÂ process called ribosomal frameshifting. Autoproteolytic cleavage of pp1a and pp1ab produces 16Â different nonstructural proteins (nsps), which together form the viral replicase complex [14]. This complex makes aÂ complementary copy of the viral RNA genome called the antigenome [(âˆ’)gRNA], which serves as aÂ template for the synthesis of new CoV genomes (replication) (Fig.Â 2). These (+)gRNA molecules, in turn, form the templates for the generation of aÂ nested set of so-called subgenomic (sg) RNAs of negative polarity [(âˆ’)sgRNAs] with aÂ common leader sequence complementary to theÂ 5â€™ (i.e. left) end of the viral genome. These negative-strand sgRNAs are subsequently transcribed to produce aÂ nested set of positive-strand sgRNAs (Figs.Â 1 andÂ 2). Translation of these (+)sgRNAs yields the major structural proteins (i.e. the S, M, E and NÂ protein in the case of SARS-CoV-2) and aÂ number of accessory proteins [15, 16]. The accessory proteins of CoVs are involved in the modulation of different cellular processes for the benefit of the virus. Most accessory proteins perform multiple functions and several of them are incorporated into virus particles as minor structural proteins [17]. The NÂ protein binds the newly synthesised positive-strand RNA genomes. The resulting ribonucleoprotein complexes (i.e. nucleocapsids) associate with membranes of the endoplasmic reticulum (ER)-Golgi intermediate compartment (ERGIC) in which the S, M and EÂ proteins are inserted. Subsequently, virus particles are formed by budding of the capsids through the ERGIC membranes (Fig.Â 2). These virus particles are then transported in vesicles (i.e. exosomes) to the surface of the cell, where they fuse with the plasma membrane, leading to the release of the newly assembled virus particles in the extracellular space (Fig.Â 2). From there they can spread to new target cells to initiate additional rounds of virus production.

COVID-19 pandemic

Since the first report on SARS-CoVâ€‘2 on 31Â December 2019 by the Wuhan Municipal Health Commission, the virus has spread rapidly across the globe, creating aÂ pandemic with aÂ colossal socioeconomic impact affecting all continents except Antarctica. As of 7Â July 2020, John Hopkins University had registered 11,626,759 confirmed cases of COVID-19 and 538,190 COVID-19-related deaths, which would correspond to aÂ global case-fatality rate of ~6%. Due to the limited testing capacity in many countries, especially at the beginning of the pandemic, and the existence of many asymptomatic and paucisymptomatic COVID-19 patients, the true incidence of SARS-CoVâ€‘2 is presumably much higher. As aÂ consequence, the true fatality rate will be lower too. Yet, excess mortality data indicate that the number of COVID-19-related deaths is also considerably higher than the reported death count (https://voxeu.org/article/excess-mortality-england-european-outlier-covid-19-pandemic), which may be caused by misdiagnosis and/or underreporting. For the well-characterised COVID-19 outbreak in February 2020 on the cruise ship Diamond Princess, case-fatality and infection-fatality rates for SARS-CoVâ€‘2 of 2.6% and 1.3%, respectively, were calculated [18]. However, these figures may also not be fully representative of the global situation because of the relatively old age of the passengers, on the one hand, and the relatively high-quality care provided to the patients, on the other. Indeed, demographic differences as well as differences in health status, health care, COVID-19 treatment and cause of death assessment (i.e. did aÂ person die with or die from COVID-19?), may explain the differences in reported case-fatality rates between different countries. By comparison, the global case-fatality rate associated with seasonal influenza epidemics is ~0.1%. The risk of COVID-19 hospital death is positively correlated with age, body-mass index and socioeconomic deprivation, e.g. in people â‰¥80Â years of age an adjusted hazard ratio (HR) of 12.64Â has been reported [19]. Males are ~2-fold more likely to die from COVID-19 than females and mortality in Caucasians is lower than in the other races [19, 20]. Recently, blood group has also been identified as aÂ risk factor for acquiring COVID-19 with respiratory failure, i.e. blood group OÂ and AÂ are associated with, respectively, aÂ lower and higher risk of acquiring severe COVID-19 than the other blood groups [21]. Most comorbidities are associated with aÂ higher risk of COVID-19 hospital death, including cardiovascular disease, diabetes, (haematological) cancer, hypertension and respiratory disease [19, 20] although after adjustment for multiple variables the association with high blood pressure was lost and with chronic heart disease was rather weak (HR 1.27) [19]. This illustrates the fact that care should be taken in interpreting the results of univariate analyses.

SARS-CoV-2 transmission

In the absence of aÂ SARS-CoVâ€‘2 vaccine and protective immunity resulting from infections with endemic human CoVs, current efforts to bring the pandemic to aÂ halt are focusing on the reduction of the basic reproduction number (R0), which is defined as the expected number of secondary cases produced by aÂ typical infected individual during the entire infectious period in aÂ completely susceptible (i.e. non-immune) population and without any deliberate actions to reduce disease transmission. Based on early transmission dynamics in Wuhan, Li etÂ al. estimated the R0 for SARS-CoVâ€‘2 to be 2.2 (95% confidence interval (CI) 1.4â€"3.9) [22].

Since COVID-19 is primarily aÂ respiratory infection, respiratory droplets produced (in decreasing numbers) by sneezing, coughing, singing, talking and breathing are the main sources of virus transmission.

AÂ treacherous aspect of SARS-CoVâ€‘2 infections is the presymptomatic transmission of the virus, which may have played an important role in the (rapid) spreading of the virus around the globe. By studying 77Â transmission pairs and based on aÂ mean incubation time of 5.2 (95% CI 4.1â€"7.0) days, He etÂ al. inferred that infectiousness started from 2.3 (95% CI 0.8â€"3.0) days before symptom onset and peaked at 0.7 (95% CI âˆ’0.2â€"2.0) days before onset of illness to gradually decline afterwards [23]. In several studies, SARS-CoVâ€‘2 RNA has been demonstrated by reverse transcription-quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR) analysis (see below) in stool and sewage, and occasionally infectious virus has been recovered from faecal samples [24, 25]. Moreover, SARS-CoVâ€‘2 has been shown to replicate in enterocytes of human small intestinal organoids [26], and the viral NÂ has been detected by immunofluorescence microscopy in the cytoplasm of gastric, duodenal and rectal epithelial cells but not in oesophageal epithelial cells of endoscopic biopsies [27]. Recently, different species of companion and farm animals including cats, dogs, ferrets, hamsters and minks have been shown to be permissive to SARS-CoVâ€‘2 infection (see, for example, [28]) and evidence has been obtained suggesting transmission of the virus from humans to these domesticated animals and vice versa (https://www.rivm.nl/en/novel-coronavirus-covid-19/pets).

Clinical manifestations

The outcome of SARS-CoVâ€‘2 infections is highly variable, which may relate to several factors including (1)Â infectious dose, (2)Â general health of the infected individual and (3)Â appropriateness of his/her immune response. AÂ large percentage (up to 81%) of people are unaware of being infected with SARS-CoVâ€‘2 [29]. Analysis of 44,415 confirmed COVID-19 cases from China as of 11Â February 2020 indicated that 81% had mild disease, 14% were severely ill and 5% developed critical illness, resulting in aÂ case-fatality rate of 2.3% [30]. In mildly affected individuals the infection remains largely restricted to the upper respiratory tract, while in seriously ill patients it subsequently spreads to the lower respiratory tract and, from there, possibly to other organs. The most common (flu-like) symptoms of COVID-19 are chills/pyrexia, dry cough, dyspnoea and fatigue or myalgia. Other, less common, symptoms include expectoration, nasal congestion/rhinorrhoea, sore throat, headache, conjunctivitis, abdominal pain, diarrhoea, nausea or vomiting, ageusia, anosmia, skin rash and discoloration of fingers or toes. These symptoms are usually mild and develop gradually (reviewed in [4, 31, 32]). More seriously affected individuals suffer from pneumonia, accompanied by progressive dyspnoea, chest pain, haemoptysis, crackles and/or respiratory insufficiency. In the most severe cases of COVID-19, the pneumonia develops into acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), which may lead directly to respiratory failure and is aÂ major cause of COVID-19-related death. The development of ARDS is thought to largely result from the hypercytokinaemia (cytokine storm) induced by the virus and, together with secondary microbial infections (e.g. due to loss of intestinal epithelial barrier integrity), can culminate in sepsis and end-organ dysfunction [33, 34]. Interestingly, manifestations of COVID-19 in children are generally less severe than in adults. The precise reason(s) for this is/are unclear. Due to the higher incidence of respiratory infections, the innate immune system of children may be â€˜trainedâ€™ and therefore better capable of mounting an efficient first line of defence against the virus, preventing the induction of aÂ cytokine storm due to vigorous replication of SARS-CoVâ€‘2 in the lower airways. Alternatively, in children replication of SARS-CoVâ€‘2 could be suppressed by the presence of other respiratory viruses. Also, airway epithelial cells of children may be less permissive to SARS-CoVâ€‘2, e.g. because of lower ACE2 expression levels [35].

The average incubation period of SARS-CoVâ€‘2 is ~5Â days with aÂ 95% CI ranging from 2Â to 14Â days [22, 36]. In aÂ study of 41Â hospitalised COVID-19 patients, 22Â developed dyspnoea with aÂ median time from illness onset to dyspnoea of 8.0 days (interquartile range (IQR) 5.0â€"13.0Â days). In the same study, medium times from onset of symptoms to hospital admission, ARDS, mechanical ventilation and admission to the intensive care unit (ICU) were 7.0 (IQR 4.0â€"8.0), 9.0 (IQR 8.0â€"14.0), 10.5 (IQR 7.0â€"14.0) and 10.5 (IQR 8.0-17.0) days, respectively [37]. In another study of 191 inpatients with laboratory-confirmed COVID-19, median times from illness onset to dyspnoea, ARDS, ICU admission and death or discharge were 7.0 (IQR 4.0â€"7.0), 12.0 (IQR 8.0â€"15.0), 12.0 (IQR 8.0â€"15.0) and 21.0 (IQR 17.0â€"25.0)Â days, respectively [38]. The median duration of virus shedding after onset of symptoms was 20.0 (IQR 16.0â€"23.0)Â days in this study.

In 6Â of these patients, radiographic abnormalities were observed before testing positive for SARS-CoVâ€‘2 RNA. Consolidation and ground-glass opacities were the dominant findings and mostly involved the periphery of the lower halves of both lungs [39]. Computed tomography (CT) scans of 81Â confirmed COVID-19 patients before and â‰¤1Â week, &gt;1â€"2Â weeks and &gt;2â€"3Â weeks after symptom onset revealed abnormalities in 2.8, 11.1, 13.0 and 12.1 of the 20Â lung segments, respectively. While the respiratory tract is the primary target of SARS-CoVâ€‘2, the clinical symptoms mentioned above show that other organs/organ systems, including the cardiovascular system, the digestive tract, the liver, the kidney, the brain and the eyes, can also be affected by COVID-19 [42â€"45]. Immune response

AÂ brief introduction to antiviral immune responses in general is provided in AppendixÂ 1 of the Electronic Supplementary Material.

In the large majority of persons, infection with SARS-CoVâ€‘2 is rapidly and efficiently controlled by the immune system and remains (largely) confined to the upper respiratory tract. As aÂ consequence, these people develop no or only mild disease. However, in aÂ small percentage of infected individuals the lower respiratory tract also becomes infected, accompanied by hyperinflammation and overactivation of the immune system, leading to excessive production of cytokines and accumulation of immune cells in the lungs. Due to the severe injury caused by the virus and by the immune system to the airway epithelial cells and the underlying endothelial cells, the alveolar-capillary barrier is broken, resulting in vascular leakage, alveolar oedema/collapse and ARDS. This may be followed by further clinical deterioration and ultimately cause death as aÂ result of multi-organ damage/failure due to secondary SARS-CoVâ€‘2 and bacterial infections and immune-mediated mechanisms.

Although detailed knowledge about the interactions between the innate immune system and SARS-CoVâ€‘2 is still scarce, innate immune responses are thought to play an important role in limiting the viral infection (to the upper respiratory tract). Based on previous research on SARS-CoV and MERS-CoV and various animal models of virus-induced acute lung injury as well as haematological and biochemical laboratory findings in COVID-19 patients, the following scenarios can be envisioned [33, 34, 48, 49]. Infection of upper airway epithelial cells, recognition/uptake of SARS-CoVâ€‘2 particles by dendritic cells and resident macrophages and activation of the complement system in the upper airways trigger the local production and release of pro-inflammatory cytokines and chemokines. These proteins induce chemotaxis of neutrophils, monocytes/macrophages, natural killer (NK) cells and lymphocytes to the site(s) of infection and participate in the activation of these immune cells. In the case of an adequate immune response, the virus infection is subsequently cleared by the concerted action of innate immune cells, the complement system, TÂ and BÂ lymphocytes and SARS-CoV-2-specific antibodies. However, when the immune response overshoots, aÂ viscous cycle is induced of further pulmonary accumulation of immune cells, production of more pro-inflammatory factors and aggravated immune-mediated lung injury.

In patients with severe COVID-19, but less so in patients with mild disease, lymphopenia is commonly observed, with drastically reduced numbers of BÂ cells, helper (i.e. CD4+) TÂ cells (THs), cytotoxic (i.e. CD8+) TÂ cells (CTLs) and NK cells. These patients also have an increased percentage of neutrophils and aÂ decreased percentage of monocytes, eosinophils and basophils ([50] and references therein). In general, neutrophil count and neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio positively correlate with disease severity and aÂ worse clinical outcome [51]. In addition, NK cells and CD8+ TÂ cells show signs of functional exhaustion, the extent of which is directly proportional to disease severity. During recovery from COVID-19, the numbers of BÂ cells, THs, CTLs and NK cells as well as exhaustion marker expression in cytotoxic lymphocytes normalise ([50] and references therein).

The specific Bâ€‘cell responses (i.e. antibody production) to SARS-CoVâ€‘2 have been studied in detail by Long etÂ al. [52] in 285 patients. At 2â€"4Â days after onset of symptoms, SARS-CoV-2-specific immunoglobulin (Ig) GÂ and/or IgM antibodies were detected in 6% of the serum samples. After 17â€"19Â days all patients had SARS-CoV-2-specific IgGs, while virus-specific IgMs reached aÂ peak of 94.1% at 20â€"22Â days after symptom onset. During the first 3Â weeks after onset of illness, SARS-CoV-2-specific IgG and IgM levels gradually increased. Severely diseased patients had significantly higher virus-specific IgG titres than mildly affected individuals at 2Â weeks after symptom onset. The median day of seroconversion for both IgG and IgM was 13Â days after onset of illness. Padoan and colleagues studied the kinetics of SARS-CoV-2-specific antibodies for 6Â weeks after the onset of fever in 19Â adult patients with RT-qPCR-confirmed COVID-19 [53]. Average SARS-CoV-2-specific IgA (i.e. mucosal antibody) and IgM levels above background started to be detected at 6 and 8Â days after the onset of COVID-19, respectively. The average level of SARS-CoV-2-specific antibodies was higher for IgA than for IgM for the whole observation period. The average IgG titre peaked at day 20â€"22Â and remained fairly constant for 1Â month, while the average IgM level peaked at 10â€"12Â days and gradually dropped thereafter.

Relatively little is known about the cellular immune response to SARS-CoVâ€‘2. Using aÂ large pool of peptides representing predicted Tâ€‘cell epitopes, SARS-CoV-2-specific CD8+ and CD4+ TÂ lymphocytes were identified in ~70Â and 100% of COVID-19 convalescent patients, respectively [54]. The majority of the THs and CTLs were directed against the highly expressed S, M and NÂ proteins of SARS-CoVâ€‘2. THs to the SÂ protein were robust and showed aÂ positive correlation with SARS-CoVâ€‘2 IgG and IgA titres. Interestingly, SARS-CoV-2-reactive CD4+ TÂ lymphocytes were detected in ~50% of unexposed individuals, indicative of possible cross-reactive Tâ€‘cell recognition between endemic CoVs and SARS-CoVâ€‘2. In another study of 14Â COVID-19 convalescent patients, variable numbers of nsp5-, Sâ€‘ and Nâ€‘specific TÂ cells were detected and aÂ direct correlation between the neutralising antibody titre and the strength of the Nâ€‘specific Tâ€‘cell response was found [55].

Diagnosis

Diagnostic assays for COVID-19 fall into two categories: (1)Â tests to detect viral components, i.e. SARS-CoVâ€‘2 RNA or protein; (2)Â assays to measure adaptive immunity (i.e. antibodies and TÂ lymphocytes) against SARS-CoVâ€‘2 infection [56, 57]. Although not specific, radiography to visualise lung disease, biomarker analysis to assess tissue/organ damage and inflammation, electron microscopy to visualise virus particles and virus infectivity assays to quantify functional virus particles can also aid in the diagnosis of COVID-19.

Virological tests

RT-qPCR-based laboratory assays on upper or lower respiratory tract samples are presently the standard tests to detect ongoing SARS-CoVâ€‘2 infections. RNA-virus detection by RT-qPCR is aÂ multistep procedure involving the extraction of RNA from the clinical specimen, the preparation of DNA copies of the extracted RNA by an enzyme called reverse transcriptase, the logarithmic amplification of viral sequences in aÂ so-called thermocycler using aÂ thermostable DNA polymerase and virus-specific DNA primers and the real-time detection of the amplification products using fluorescence-based methods (see, for example, https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ThG_02miq-4 for an illustrative movie).

The preferred specimens for the detection of SARS-CoVâ€‘2 RNA by RT-qPCR are nasopharyngeal samples collected with aÂ flocked swap and preserved in an appropriate transport medium. Besides in airway samples, SARS-CoVâ€‘2 RNA is also frequently detected in faeces (reflecting infection of the gastrointestinal tract), occasionally in blood samples (indicative of viraemia) and rarely in urine.

An alternative method to confirm active infections is by aÂ sandwich enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA; Fig.Â 3a). This assay detects viral protein (i.e. antigen) in clinical specimens after lysis of the cells and virus particles in the sample. The resulting solution is applied to aÂ well coated with aÂ so-called capture antibody specific for one of the virion proteins. Next, an antibody recognising another epitope of the same virion protein (the so-called detection antibody) is applied to the well, followed by an enzyme-conjugated third antibody directed against the detection antibody. AÂ colourless substrate is then added to the well, which can be converted by the enzyme into aÂ coloured product that is detected with aÂ colorimeter. The amount of coloured product that is generated correlates with the amount of virus present in the clinical specimen. Since aÂ sandwich ELISA is aÂ laborious procedure requiring multiple washing steps after each addition, which are usually performed automatically, this method is unsuitable for point-of-care testing. This has led to the development of immunochromatography strips for fast instrument-free testing employing capillary movement (Fig.Â 3b). These strips contain aÂ sample pad for specimen application followed by aÂ conjugate pad containing an antigen-specific antibody linked to, for example, colloidal gold or latex particles (first antibody). If the sample contains antigens, complexes are formed with the first antibody, which migrate to the so-called test line, where aÂ fraction of these complexes are bound by another antigen-specific antibody covalently attached to the strip (second antibody), resulting in the formation of aÂ coloured line. The remaining complexes and non-complexed first antibodies migrate further until they reach the so-called control line, which contains covalently attached antibodies recognising the first antibody (third antibody). This results in the formation of aÂ second coloured line, irrespective of the presence of antigen in the clinical specimen.

Different studies report somewhat different times after the onset of symptoms at which the first antibodies against SARS-CoVâ€‘2 proteins are detected. In theory, SARS-CoVâ€‘2 could be completely eradicated by strict isolation of all infectious sources similar to the extinction of aÂ predator in the absence of prey. Vaccines

This has created aÂ huge incentive for the development of aÂ safe and effective SARS-CoVâ€‘2 vaccine (see https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/16DbPhF9OD0MHHtCR12of6yUcfiRzP_-XGkynEbnipds/edit#gid=1804775590 for an overview) to block infection or, at least, mitigate the consequences of infection. AÂ COVID-19 vaccine does not necessarily have to contain/express SARS-CoVâ€‘2 proteins. Despite the urgent need for aÂ SARS-CoVâ€‘2 vaccine, the safety aspects should not be neglected. Nonetheless, active immunisation using an effective vaccine in combination with aÂ rigorous surveillance and containment programme can lead to the complete eradication of aÂ viral disease, as demonstrated for smallpox. Which type(s) of vaccine will eventually be used to combat COVID-19 will be determined by aÂ combination of factors including safety, efficacy and time-to-market introduction.

Antivirals

Rather than trying to prevent the development of SARS-CoV-2-related sickness by arming the immune system through vaccination, another strategy consists of thwarting the virus once an infection has revealed itself by the administration of antiviral drugs to the patient. Due to (1)Â the lack of approved drugs directed against human CoVs and (2)Â the long times involved in the development and evaluation of new antiviral agents, the main focus is currently on repositioning existing drugs designed for other (viral) diseases to treat COVID-19 patients [65]. In addition, unapproved drugs showing antiviral activity in animal models of SARS-CoV and MERS-CoV are being tested for the ability to inhibit SARS-CoVâ€‘2 replication. Mechanistically, anti-SARS-CoVâ€‘2 drugs exert their beneficial effect by inhibiting crucial steps in the viral life cycle like receptor binding, envelope fusion, polyprotein processing and RNA-dependent RNA synthesis (Fig.Â 5; [60, 63]).

Recombinant soluble human ACE2 (sACE2) is being evaluated for its ability to block the interaction between SARS-CoVâ€‘2 and target cells. sACE2 could also compensate for the SARS-CoV-2-related reduction of surface ACE2 levels and consequential disturbance of angiotensin II/angiotensin-(1â€"7)Â balance [66] and thereby help to preserve pulmonary vascular integrity. Other pharmacological targets could be nsp3 and nsp5, the papain- and chymotrypsin-like proteases encoded by ORF 1a and involved in the processing of the SARS-CoVâ€‘2 polyproteins that produce the viral replication and transcription complexes (Figs.Â 1 andÂ 2). The in vivo effectiveness of these drugs against SARS-CoVâ€‘2 remains to be seen as the HIV protease belongs to the family of aspartyl proteases and nsp3 and nsp5 are cysteine proteases.

AÂ highly specific way to inhibit SARS-CoVâ€‘2 would be by RNA interference. This method is based on the delivery to virus-infected/target cells of small (modified) RNA molecules (so-called small interfering RNAs or siRNAs) that are binding/complementary to the viral genome and promote its degradation. By simultaneous treatment of COVID-19 with multiple siRNAs targeting different parts of the SARS-CoVâ€‘2 genome, it will be very hard for the virus to escape from this antiviral mechanism by changing its genetic code through mutation.

An alternative to the inhibition of viral proteins/processes is the suppression of host cell factors/processes required by the virus. Obvious targets are the SARS-CoVâ€‘2 attachment receptor ACE2 and the host proteases involved in the processing of the SÂ protein to render it fusion competent. However, care should be taken that antagonists of these host proteins do not interfere with vital physiological processes. Early activation of cellular defence systems could also be an effective means to suppress SARS-CoVâ€‘2 replication. One possibility would be to treat patients with interferons, as these cytokines play an important role in the (1)Â development of early innate immune responses to viral infections, (2)Â activation of subsequent adaptive immune responses and (3)Â dampening of immunopathogenic mechanisms [70]. Support for this approach is provided by the finding that SARS-CoV and MERS-CoV counteract interferon signalling by multiple mechanisms and that replication of these viruses can be inhibited by interferons [15, 63, 71]. There may, however, also be aÂ downside to treatment of COVID-19 with interferons given the recent finding that they can increase ACE2 expression in human airway epithelial cells [72].

In general, in patients at risk of developing severe disease following infection with SARS-CoVâ€‘2, antiviral drug therapy should be started as soon as possible to minimise virus-induced damage of airway epithelium and to avoid the subsequent development of hypercytokinaemia. In addition, COVID-19 patients receiving antiviral drugs must be closely monitored for possible adverse effects and potential interactions with other drugs should be reckoned with. Similar to what has been found for HIV and hepatitis CÂ virus, combining different antiviral drugs may have additive or synergistic effects. Antiviral drug cocktails will also lower the risk of the selection of SARS-CoVâ€‘2 variants with mutations that resist antiviral drug therapy. Antiviral drugs may also be employed as prophylactics to prevent infection or to reduce virus shedding and thereby the risk of virus spreading.

Anticoagulants

Multiple recent studies have identified venous and arterial thrombosis secondary to SARS-CoVâ€‘2 infection as aÂ major complication of COVID-19, which likely contributes to its relatively high mortality rate [77, 78]. Cardiovascular manifestations

As already mentioned above, albeit patients with pre-existing cardiovascular disease (CVD) may not be more susceptible to contracting SARS-CoVâ€‘2 than healthy individuals, they have aÂ higher chance of developing severe COVID-19 and are more likely to die from SARS-CoVâ€‘2 infection. The higher COVID-19-related mortality of CVD patients may be directly related to or coincident with their cardiovascular comorbidities. Although COVID-19 primarily disrupts the respiratory system, the disease frequently affects the cardiovascular system as well, especially in more severe cases. The currently available data suggest that some 20â€"30% of hospitalised COVID-19 patients display cardiac complications [84], and in aÂ recent study of 671 severely ill COVID-19 patients acute myocardial injury and acute heart failure were identified as death-related complications in 30.6% and 19.4% of the 62Â deceased patients, respectively [85]. Of these patients, 98.4% had ARDS and 90.3% suffered from acute respiratory failure. AÂ small percentage of hospitalised COVID-19 patients develop cardiac disease in the absence of marked pulmonary illness [86].

The cardiovascular complications of COVID-19 include acute myocardial injury, heart failure with or without cardiogenic shock, pericardial effusion with or without tamponade, arrhythmias and sudden cardiac death and thrombosis of small and large blood vessels [31, 87].

The most common cardiac complication of COVID-19 is acute myocardial injury, as evinced by increased plasma levels of cardiac troponin and echo-/electrocardiographic abnormalities. In aÂ retrospective study of 416 inpatients, 19.7% displayed cardiac injury defined as high-sensitivity cardiac troponinÂ I (hs-cTnI) levels above the 99thÂ percentile of aÂ healthy reference population. The mortality rate among patients with elevated hs-cTnI levels was 51.2% versus 4.5% in patients without myocardial injury [88]. The myocardial injury could also be caused by virus- or immune-mediated myocarditis, stress-induced cardiomyopathy or oxygen supply and demand mismatch resulting in type 2Â myocardial infarction (i.e. myocardial infarction without acute coronary syndrome), possible linked to respiratory insufficiency and/or sepsis. Sepsis and the sepsis-related cytokine storm together with myocardial injury and ischaemia are likely involved in the occurrence of cardiac arrhythmias in COVID-19 patients. The cytokine storm is also thought to be largely responsible for the hypercoagulable state and consequential disseminated intravascular coagulation /thrombotic microangiopathy and venous thrombosis including pulmonary embolism often seen in (severely ill/deceased) COVID-19 patients. Other factors contributing to the coagulopathy could be endothelial damage due to (1)Â the hyperinflammatory response typical of severe COVID-19 cases, (2)Â disturbance of the angiotensin-2/angiotensin-(1â€"7)Â balance and (3)Â SARS-CoVâ€‘2 infection of endothelial cells [81].

More extensive discussions about the cardiovascular aspects of COVID-19 are provided in some recent reviews [87, 90, 91]. Conclusion

After SARS-CoV and MERS-CoV, SARS-CoVâ€‘2 is the third zootic betacoronavirus of the 21stÂ century that imposes aÂ serious threat to human health. The outcome of aÂ SARS-CoVâ€‘2 infection is highly variable and dependent on multiple factors, including SARS-CoVâ€‘2 genotype, infectious dose, genetic background, health status, immunological readiness and age. While COVID-19 is primarily aÂ respiratory disease and mostly has aÂ mild course, in severe cases other organs are also affected, including the heart and vasculature. To what extent the cardiovascular manifestations of COVID-19 are due to direct viral injury of cardiovascular cells or are an indirect consequence of the extensive lung pathology and accompanying (hyper)inflammation remains to be determined. The COVID-19 pandemic has once again alerted us to the high adaptive potential of RNA viruses and the downsides of globalisation. At the same time, international collaboration between medical professionals, scientists and politicians will be instrumental in tackling the current and foreseeable future (corona)virus outbreaks.
