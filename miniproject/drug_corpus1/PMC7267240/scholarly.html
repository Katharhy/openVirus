PHYSIOLOGICAL ATTRIBUTES OF SALIVA

Human saliva is a unique body fluid secreted by the salivary glands. It is mainly composed of water (94%â€"99%), with organic molecules accounting for approximately 0.5% and inorganic molecules for 0.2% (Zhang etÂ al.,Â 2016). It has the functions of lubricating oral mucosa, digesting food, cleaning and protecting the oral cavity, and is one of the most important factors affecting homeostasis of the oral cavity (Helmerhorst &amp; Oppenheim,Â 2007). A normal adult secretes approximately 600Â ml of saliva every day (Zhang etÂ al.,Â 2016). In addition to salivary gland excreta, saliva also contains food residue, serum components, oral microorganisms, and their metabolites, exfoliated epithelial cells and white blood cells (Proctor etÂ al.,Â 2018).

BIOLOGICAL EFFECTS OF SALIVA ON MICROORGANISMS

So far, more than 700 microbial species, many of which are related to oral and systemic diseases, have been identified in saliva. Saliva not only provides ecological niche for the colonization and growth of oral microorganisms, but also inhibits the overâ€�growth of specific pathogens to maintain the homeostasis of oral cavity.

The salivary acquired pellicle that consist of salivary proteins such as acid prolineâ€�rich protein, statherin and histatins, allow the oral bacteria to colonize on tooth surfaces, and provide nutrition for their survival, reproduction and metabolism (Kolenbrander,Â 2011). Salivary mucins and glycosylated proteins can provide carbon and nitrogen sources for the growth and metabolism of oral microbiota. The binding between salivary proteins and oral microbes can not only help microbial adherence, but also cause the aggregation of oral microbiota and thus increase the clearance of bacteria from the oral cavity, including some pathogens. Therefore, saliva can act as a â€œgatekeeperâ€�, and help prevent the spread of pathogens to the respiratory and gastrointestinal tracts (Ruhl,Â 2012).

In addition to providing a nutritional source for the growth of microorganisms, saliva contains a large number of antibacterial and antiviral proteins. The major antimicrobial components that have been identified in saliva include lysozyme, peroxidase, lactoferrin, and histatins (Tenovuo,Â 2002). Secretory IgA (sIgA) plays an important role in immunity by binding the surface molecules of pathogenic microorganisms and preventing adhesion and colonization (Balmaseda etÂ al.,Â 2003). Of interest, most of these antibacterial proteins, including cathelcidin (LLâ€�37), lactoferrin, lysozyme, mucins, peroxidase, salivary agglutinin (gp340, DMBT1), sIgA, SLPI, and Î±, Î² defensins, also display antiviral activities, typically against specific viral pathogens (Malamud etÂ al.,Â 2011). The mechanisms of viral inactivation include not only direct binding to the virus, but also indirect methods via intracellular modulation of viral replication, modulation of signaling pathways, and recruitment of immune cells that contribute to antiviral activity in vivo (Diamond, Beckloff, &amp; Ryan,Â 2008).

SALIVA AS A POTENTIAL SOURCE OF VIRUS SPREAD

The interaction between viruses and saliva is a complex biological process. Human saliva is abundant of biologically active components, such as prolineâ€�rich proteins, mucins MG1 and MG2, and gp340. These components interact with pathogens and cause multiple influences on their biological behavior (de Almeida Pdel, Gregio, Machado, de Lima, &amp; Azevedo,Â 2008). Moreover, pathogenic viruses in saliva have altered biological patterns such as increased aggregation and communicability (Anschau &amp; Sanjuan,Â 2020). According to a previous study on vesicular stomatitis virus, this pathogen has a strong aggregating ability in the presence of saliva, and some host factors, such as fibrinogen, could promote this salivaâ€�induced aggregation process, suggesting the important role saliva plays in the biological behavior of this virus (Anschau &amp; Sanjuan,Â 2020). According to another study, the biochemical components of saliva are closely associated with the presence of Zika virus (ZIKV), indicating complex interactions between the virus and saliva (Siqueira, Moffa, Mussi, &amp; Machado,Â 2016). Hence investigation of the biological functions of saliva on the behavior of viruses, and the impact of virus on the composition of saliva, suggest the transmission of virus can be closely connected with saliva.

4.2
Other viruses

Apart from coronavirus, human saliva also plays a role in the pathogenicity of other viruses. CONCLUSIONS

The pandemic of COVIDâ€�19 should reinforce the awareness of dental professionals on the transmission risk during dental practice.
