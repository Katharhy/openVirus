DISCUSSION

Coinfection with other pathogens has been described in up to 40% of children with COVIDâ€�19, including influenza A and B, mycoplasma, respiratory syncytial virus, and cytomegalovirus.4 This is the first report of coinfection with MTB. Airway obstruction due to MTB is known to be complicated by a viral infection, causing additional inflammation, thereby worsening the obstruction.5

BCG vaccination confers decreased risk of severe childhood TB disease in highâ€�burden areas, an effect negated by low vaccine coverage.6 Besides protection against TB, BCG vaccination also modulates the immune response to subsequent infections caused by other pathogens, including viruses, by the induction of innate immunity and heterologous lymphocyte activation.7, 8 In this case, the question raised is whether lack of BCG vaccination increased susceptibility to severe pulmonary TB, COVIDâ€�19, and possibly other viral coinfection. The protective effects of BCG vaccination against severity and poor outcome in COVIDâ€�19 infection is currently a subject of clinical trials.9

This case highlights the difficulty that diagnosis and management of COVIDâ€�19 presents to clinicians in resourceâ€�constrained countries with high TB and HIV burden. This child, known with PTB, presented with clinical symptoms that could have been presumed to be related due to either TB or COVIDâ€�19. Routine management of children with significant airway obstruction is also compromised due to the risk bronchoscopy may hold. The nonspecific clinical and radiological findings of COVIDâ€�19 in children complicate recognition. Computed tomography (CT) findings are thought to be more specific,4 but in TB endemic countries, CT scans are not easily available. In patients with complicated PTB, with extensive parenchymal involvement, it would be impossible to differentiate from COVIDâ€�19, based on symptoms and chest radiograph findings alone. Bronchoscopy has been used to confirm and determine the severity of tuberculous airway obstruction in children, guiding the decision either to treat medically with antituberculous drugs and oral prednisone or intervene surgically.10 As bronchoscopy is a very highâ€�risk aerosolâ€�generating medical procedure, in TB endemic areas, this has to be performed with strict indications and with full personal protective equipment (PPE).11 The indications for bronchoscopy in pediatric TB may need to be revised as full PPE, although optimal, is not always available in a resourceâ€�constrained world and may be limited to only N95 masks.

Currently, it would be very difficult to predict whether PTB in children confers additional risk of developing severe COVIDâ€�19. This case illustrates that children with confirmed PTB may have persistent or worsening symptoms due to other viral infections, including COVIDâ€�19 infection, and may present with new pneumonia and exacerbation of TBâ€�associated nodal airway obstruction. However, the rapid improvement is reassuring. The child experienced shortâ€�term deterioration of his airway obstruction due to COVIDâ€�19, which subsequently rapidly improved. Oral prednisone at 2â€‰mg/kg for TB airway obstruction was continued for 1â€‰month even after recovery from COVIDâ€�19.

COVIDâ€�19 may result in deterioration in children with TB. More data on the effect of COVIDâ€�19 on presentation and treatment response in children with TB (including the full spectrum) is needed.
