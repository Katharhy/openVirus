Abstract
Summary

The coronavirus disease 2019 (Covidâ€�19) pandemic, caused by severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2), is an international public health crisis with devastating effects. INTRODUCTION

In December 2019, pneumonia cases of unknown etiology were reported in Wuhan district of Hubei Province, China.1 By January 7, 2020, the causative agent of the disease was identified as the 2019 novel coronavirus (2019â€�nCoV).2 The virus was later renamed as severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2), and hence the disease was termed coronavirus disease 2019 (Covidâ€�19).3 On January 30, 2020, the World Health Organization (WHO) announced Covidâ€�19 as a Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC), and 2â€‰months later, on March 11, it was declared as a global pandemic.3, 4, 5 SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 is primarily transmitted from personâ€�toâ€�person through droplets while coughing and sneezing, enabling the virus to disperse upto 1 to 2 m.6, 7, 8 The transmission occurs not only from symptomatic and preâ€�symptomatic patients but also probably from those who are asymptomatic,9, 10, 11, 12, 13 with the incubation period ranging from 2 to 14â€‰days.3, 6 There is no specific antiviral for Covidâ€�19 but multiple drugs are being assessed in clinical trials.14, 15, 16, 17

Dengue is a major public health problem throughout tropical and subâ€�tropical regions.18 This disease is caused by any of the four serotypes of dengue virus (DENV), transmitted by mosquitoes, primarily the Aedes aegypti species and the development of a efficacious vaccine still remains a challenging task19, 20 Dengue is also a rapidly increasing problem among international travelers21, 22, 23, 24 and it is the leading cause of febrile illness in returning travelers from Asia.25, 26 In Asia, dengue is endemic and there has been a 400% increase in the number of dengue cases over the last 13â€‰years (2000â€�2013), with the World Health Organization (WHO) estimating there to be 100 million symptomatic cases and 300 million asymptomatic cases, annually.27 Singapore, a Southeast Asian country, is experiencing a dengue outbreak that has been ongoing since April 9, 2020; there were 5091 cases with 98 active clusters.28 Dengue outbreaks also occurred in Indonesia where 68â€‰000 dengue cases have been reported across the nation as of June 21, 2020, resulting in 446 deaths.29 The dengue cases were reported in 460 districts in Indonesia of which 439 of them have also reported cases of Covidâ€�19.30 Dengue outbreaks have occurred in other Asian countries as well.31

Clinical symptoms of Covidâ€�19 include cough, muscle aches, fatigue, skin rash, and petechiae, making it challenging to differentiate Covidâ€�19 from other endemic viral infections in the region, such as dengue,32 and thus potentially leading to misdiagnosis.33 In addition, a study in Singapore reported the possibility of serological crossâ€�reactivity between SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 and DENV,34 raising the question of overlapping immunological cascades between Covidâ€�19, dengue, and other arboviruses. This article aims to: (a) provide evidence of Covidâ€�19 and dengue misdiagnosis in clinical settings and the possibility of crossâ€�reactivity between SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 and DENV; (b) discuss the possible consequence of immune response overlapping on enhancement or protective effect, disease severity and vaccine development; and (c) provide insights for further studies that are urgently needed in the region.

COVIDâ€�19 PANDEMIC AND ITS EFFECT ON DENGUE IN ASIA

Coâ€�infection and coâ€�occurrence of Covidâ€�19 and dengue have introduced a significant burden on healthcare systems in dengueâ€�endemic regions.35, 36, 37, 38, 39 The complexity of diverse disease severities, prolonged infectious periods, and shared clinical manifestations and pathogenesis have made their diagnosis, treatment, and resource allocation challenging, particularly in developing countries in Asia with high prevalence of dengue and other arboviruses.

2.1
Misdiagnosis between dengue and Covidâ€�19

A study consisting of more than 44â€‰000 Covidâ€�19 cases in China found that 81% of the cases exhibited mild to moderate symptoms.40 Some patients reported complaints of fever, cough, muscle aches, or fatigue with normal chest radiography, resembling other viral infections, thereby making it is difficult for doctors to differentiate Covidâ€�19 from dengue, which leads to primary examination misdiagnosis.32, 41 Further, clinical manifestations on the skin, such as rashes and petechiae, have been reported in Covidâ€�19 patients,42 which are also commonly found in dengue.33 A study in Italy found that Covidâ€�19 patients presented with skin erythematous, rash, urticaria, and vesicles.43 Skin manifestations have also been reported in Madrid.44 Such skin manifestations, including rash or petechiae, increase the challenge in differentiating between dengue and Covidâ€�19 in dengueâ€�endemic regions.33, 45 Misdiagnosis between Covidâ€�19 and dengue based on clinical presentation was also reported in Thailand, where patients presented with petechiae and thrombocytopenia, were diagnosed as dengue but were later diagnosed with Covidâ€�19 after developing respiratory symptoms and undergoing reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (RTâ€�PCR) testing.33, 46 In addition, a study of 41 Covidâ€�19 patients reported the occurrence of hematological disorders, such as leukopenia, decreased platelet count40 and prothrombin time.45

Beyond the similar clinical manifestations of Covidâ€�19 and dengue, misdiagnosis may be due to serological crossâ€�reactivity between SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 and DENV. It was hypothesized that patients with previous exposure to DENV possess antiâ€�DENV antibodies that are crossâ€�reactive with SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 antigens.32 Alternatively, there may be antigenic similarities between SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 and DENV, such that upon SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 infection, the body is triggered to generate antiâ€�DENV antibodies derived from memory B cells.32 These possibilities might have caused the falseâ€�positive phenomena in rapid dengue serology tests.

Misdiagnosis due to serology crossâ€�reactivity between Covidâ€�19 and dengue was first reported in Singapore where patients were initially confirmed to have dengue through dengue IgM and IgG rapid serological testing, but were later found to be positive for Covidâ€�19 using RTâ€�PCR testing.34 Subsequent RTâ€�PCR analysis were failed to detect DENV in both urine and blood samples, confirming the absence of dengue infection in the respective patients. This suggests the possibility of serological crossâ€�reactivity between DENV and SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2, in particular when using rapid serologyâ€�based test, that could lead to significant challenges in dengueâ€�endemic countries in Asia. In Indonesia for example, there have been no reports of misdiagnoses between Covidâ€�19 and dengue in the literature. However, there has been an increase in dengue cases in the country; 68â€‰000 cases have been reported as of June 21, 2020.29 In Indonesia, most dengue cases were exclusively diagnosed with IgM and IgG rapid serological testing, if not clinically diagnosed.47, 48 Therefore, it is possible that there were Covidâ€�19 cases among these dengueâ€�diagnosed patients. In addition, it remains challenging to access Covidâ€�19 testing in Indonesia, as reported early in the pandemic.49 The lack of adequate laboratories across the country and low testing rate could hamper the diagnosis and management of Covidâ€�19 patients.

The wide range of Covidâ€�19 clinical symptoms and lack of a specific and affordable test to differentiate Covidâ€�19 and dengue have led to serious impacts on community health in the dengueâ€�endemic countries. The WHO has reported that the antigenâ€�based rapid diagnostic test (RDT) for Covidâ€�19 has a sensitivity of 34% to 80%.50 Therefore, there is an urgent need for an affordable and accurate diagnostic method to prevent misdiagnosis of Covidâ€�19 and dengue in Asian countries with limited resources.

2.2
Shared pathophysiology between Covidâ€�19 and dengue

Covidâ€�19 and dengue exhibit some pathophysiological similarities, such as capillary leakage, thrombocytopenia, and coagulopathy (Figure 1).39 Plasma leakage is a crucial factor for dengue pathophysiology and is primarily mediated by the host immunological response. Several immunoâ€�mediators, including proâ€�inflammatory cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor (TNF), interleukinâ€�6 (ILâ€�6), interferon gamma (IFNâ€�Î³), and chemokines such as macrophage migration inhibitory factor (MIF), are associated with plasma leakage.51, 52 Altered platelet function53 and increased Câ€�reactive protein (CRP) levels54, 55 are also responsible for plasma leakage. Covidâ€�19 is characterized by overâ€�activation of effector Tâ€�cell function and increased inflammatory cytokine production, especially ILâ€�6, often leading to a cytokine storm.56 ILâ€�6, along with other inflammatory mediators, such as ILâ€�1, TNF, and IFNâ€�Î³, contribute not only to plasma leakage but also to other vascular disorders, including vascular permeability and disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC).57 In dengue, plasma leakage is associated with the interaction between nonstructural protein 1 (NS1)â€�specific antibodies and proteins expressed on endothelial cell surfaces, that may pave the way for the elevated rate of viral replication and inflammatory cytokine secretion.58, 59, 60 However, the mechanism of capillary leakage in Covidâ€�19 is not well understood and further studies are required to elucidate it.

Recent studies have demonstrated that the severity or progression of Covidâ€�19 is positively associated with plasma CRP levels.61, 62, 63 CRP is also important for dengue progression and may potentially be used as a prognostic biomarker.64, 65, 66, 67 Hepatocytes primarily synthesize CRP upon stimulation through inflammation. CRP then binds various pathogens and activates the complement system through the classical pathway.68

Thrombocytopenia is one of the main characteristics of DENV infection.69, 70, 71, 72, 73 In dengue, NS1 induces complementâ€�mediated pathways, which cause platelet lysis.74 DENV infected endothelial cells exhibit higher expression of Eâ€�selectin and Pâ€�selectin and increase the interaction of platelets with monocytes, leukocytes, and endothelium, which contribute to thrombocytopenia.75, 76, 77, 78 In addition, elevated levels of plateletâ€�associated immunoglobulin (PAIgM or PAIgG) are reported in dengue cases and are also correlated with thrombocytopenia.74 Thrombocytopenia is also observed in Covidâ€�19 patients.79, 80, 81 A metaâ€�analysis found that thrombocytopenia is associated with the severity of Covidâ€�19.79 SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 infection has been speculated to lead to endothelial damage and interfere with the activity of certain receptors to inhibit bone marrow growth and induce apoptosis, leading to abnormal hematopoiesis and ultimately resulting in thrombocytopenia.82 Moreover, immune systemâ€�mediated specific destruction of platelets has also been observed in Covidâ€�19 patients, which may arise from the elevated levels of immune complexes and autoimmune antibodies.83 It is hypothesized that the cytokine storm damages the bone marrow hematopoietic progenitor cells, resulting in a decline in primary platelet production and a decline in platelet count in peripheral blood.83 However, these speculations must to be investigated and verified in the context of SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 infection.

Coagulopathy is one of the main pathologies associated with DENV infection84, 85 and is also presented in cases of Covidâ€�19.86, 87, 88 Both SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 and dengue infections exhibit prolonged prothrombin time and partial thromboplastin time.89, 90 Surprisingly, the activity of coagulation factors, including prothrombin, factors V, VII, VIII, IX, and X, antithrombin, and Î±2â€�antiplasmin, was markedly reduced during the acute febrile stage of dengue; while in contrast, it appears to increase in the Covidâ€�19 patients.91 Growing evidence suggests that hypercoagulable state was evident in Covidâ€�19 patients. This condition leads to the increasing incidence of venous and arterial thromboembolic diseases such as pulmonary embolism, venous thromboembolism, myocardial infarction, stroke, and other microvascular thrombosis (Figure 1).2, 92, 93, 94, 95, 96 The pathobiology of coagulopathy in Covidâ€�19 is not well understood. A recent study reported the presence of antiphospholipid antibodies in Covidâ€�19 patients, including anticardiolipin IgA antibodies and antiâ€�Î²2â€�glycoprotein I IgA and IgG antibodies, which abnormally target several phosphoproteins.89 The presence of these antiphospholipid antibodies is considered associated with coagulopathy.97 However, several critical illnesses and other infections also elevated the levels of these antibodies; therefore, it is speculated that the presence of these antibodies might be less associated with the occurrence of any severe coagulopathies.98 Studies have also reported the presence of DIC preâ€�terminally in Covidâ€�19 patients.86, 87, 88

2.3
Dengue and Covidâ€�19: The role of antibodyâ€�dependent enhancement in disease progression

The pathogenesis of Covidâ€�19 may include antibodyâ€�dependent enhancement (ADE).99 Mechanistically, ADE involves the crossâ€�linking of virus and antibody or virus and activated complement component complexes through interactions with cellular molecules, such as Fc and complement receptors or cell surface molecules, to promote internalization of the virus and increase the infection of monocytic and granulocytic cells.100, 101, 102 Although the role of ADE has been reported in numerous contemporary viral diseases affecting humans and animals,103, 104, 105 dengue is arguably the best disease model to study this phenomenon.106 DENV homotypic infection confers longâ€�lasting and even lifeâ€�time immunity. However, crossâ€�neutralizing antibodies against reâ€�infection of different serotypes are shortâ€�lived.107, 108 At subâ€�neutralizing concentrations, these heterotypic antibodies may trigger ADE and are associated with higher odds of more severe forms of secondary infection when present at relatively low levels in children.109 This condition affects the development of a dengue vaccine, given that vaccination is considered to mimic a primary infection. Indeed, the diminishing levels of antibodies may pose a serious risk of ADE occurrence in children with secondary infections110 and may cause alert for vaccineâ€�induced enhancement of viral infection.111, 112, 113

Approximately 40â€�45% of SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 infection may be asymptomatic,114 despite clinical findings explaining the pathological mechanism of Covidâ€�19 as both directly cytotoxic and immuneâ€�mediated.115 SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 infection induces an exuberant inflammatory response, known as a cytokine storm,116 which subsequently results in uncontrolled pulmonary inflammation primed by rapid viral replication, cellular damage, virusâ€�induced downregulation of angiotensin converting enzyme 2 (ACE2), viral shedding, and ADE.117 In coronaviruses, ADE may function through the interaction of Fc, or other receptors in susceptible cells, with the complex between virus and antiâ€�spike (S) proteinâ€�neutralizing antibodies, thereby facilitating both inflammatory responses and persistent viral replication in the lungs.102, 117, 118 ADE have been reported in severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus (SARSâ€�CoV) infection and has been observed in vaccineâ€�induced infections by a SARSâ€�CoV vaccine through an Fc gamma receptor II (FcÎ³RII)â€�dependent mechanism.119 Polymorphic FcÎ³RIIA on monocytes has been reported to favor ligation of nonâ€�neutralizing antibodyâ€�virus complexes in both dengue and Covidâ€�19. In dengue, this ligation activates the expression of negative regulators of dihydroxyacetone kinase and autophagy proteins (cascade of downâ€�regulatory pathways) to decrease production of type I IFNâ€�activated antiviral molecules and suppress the capacity of macrophage antiviral mechanisms, leading to enhanced viral replication.120 FcÎ³RIIAâ€�mediated enhancement has been reported in SARSâ€�CoV in a study of 180 people from Hong Kong who were infected with SARSâ€�CoV. Moreover, it was found that FcÎ³RIIA polymorphism affects disease severity.121

ADE of SARSâ€�CoV is thought to utilize a novel cell entry mechanism for entry into immune cells, wherein antibodyâ€�mediated infection is dependent on FcÎ³RII but does not utilize the endosomal/lysosomal pathway through the ACE2 receptor.122 Viral neutralization was demonstrated with concentrated antisera against the S protein, although higher dilutions failed to neutralize the infective virus and even induced apoptosis caused by increased viral infection. In contrast, antiâ€�nucleocapsid (N) protein antibodies neither neutralized the virus, nor caused ADE. However, nucleocapsid proteins have been observed to induce cytotoxic T lymphocyte (CTL) immune responses that are specifically SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 protective.118

The modulation of pathogenesis among diseases caused by viruses from the same family has been previously described, such as between Zika and dengue.113, 123 Prior infections with other coronaviruses, ranging from those that cause the common cold to SARS, are thought to have primed Covidâ€�19, and might lead to the development of severe SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 infection.99 However, the molecular and immunological host response to SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 infection have not yet been fully elucidated to confirm ADE.124 In the tropical areas of the world, the apparent crossâ€�reactivity between antibodies in dengue and Covidâ€�19 serology tests have been reported.125 A study in Colombia found that there was a decreasing trend of dengue during increasing reports of Covidâ€�19 and it was speculated it might due to viral interference of SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 over DENV.126 Studies are needed to identify the interaction between these antibodies against dengue and Covidâ€�19. How will Covidâ€�19 pathogenesis in populations where the immunities to DENV or other arboviruses are present? Furthermore, the mechanism of ADE in viral infection has become a great concern to disease control by vaccination. Therefore, the development of SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 vaccines has to incorporate approaches to develop the vaccine with minimum or no risk for ADE, if ADE exists in COVIDâ€�19.

2.4
Covidâ€�19 and dengue crossâ€�reactivity: Consequences for Covidâ€�19 vaccine development

Vaccine development must account for safety measures, including immuneâ€�related adverse events (irAE) that may result from vaccination. ADE leading to a cytokine storm is a hallmark of SARSâ€�CoV pathogenesis and disease severity,127 and of dengue infection.109 This may also be the case for SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2.28 ADE has been suggested to be caused by preâ€�existing, nonâ€�neutralizing, lowâ€�affinity antibodies,111 along with neutralizing antibody128; therefore, the antigen design of a vaccine must ensure correct antigen conformation129 or spikeâ€�based subunit vaccines lacking the receptor binding domain (RBD).128

CONCLUSION, RECOMMENDATIONS AND FURTHER PERSPECTIVE

As dengueâ€�endemic region, some countries in Asia experiencing of overlapping outbreaks of dengue and Covidâ€�19. This poses a challenge for accurate diagnosis and treatment since both infections share similar symptoms and laboratory features in the early phase. In addition, crossâ€�reactivity between antibodies against DENV and SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 serology tests have been documented in reports. Therefore, a simple and affordable rapid test capable of differentiating SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 and DENV with high sensitivity, is urgently needed. In addition, there is an urgent need to establish additional laboratories to perform specific RTâ€�PCR testing for SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 in the region.

Although some studies have speculated the possible interaction between SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 and DENV126 and the possible of ADE in SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 infection,134 no strong evidence yet to support this and further studies are warranted to elucidate this association. There is minimal analysis of the immunological differences in the mechanisms of enhancement and differences in receptor binding viral proteins among two genetically diverse RNA viruses such as DENV and SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2. Detailed receptor sequence analysis would be required to substantiate the possibility of crossâ€�reactivity between DENV and SARSâ€�CoVâ€�2 antibodies and to establish the immunological relations between the antibodies elicited by DENV infection and their impact on Covidâ€�19 and vice versa.

Other perspectives that need to be explored are the role of vector control and changes of DENV serotype circulating during the Covidâ€�19 pandemic in the region. During the pandemic, vector control measures might have decreased in the community and dengue prevention programs might have been paused in some countries. Therefore, countries that are experiencing or have high risk for dengue outbreak should maintain the vector control measures during the pandemic.135 One of the most possible strategies is to improve withinâ€�household strategies by encouraging community members to reduce mosquitoes breeding sites in and around the homes. Lastly, displacement of DENV serotypes or genotypes leading to the cyclic pattern of dengue have been reported in several countries in Asia previously.47, 48, 136, 137, 138 This could be another possible reason for the observed increase of dengue cases during Covidâ€�19 pandemic. Continuous dengue surveillance needs to be in placed to track transmission dynamics of DENV infection in the region.
